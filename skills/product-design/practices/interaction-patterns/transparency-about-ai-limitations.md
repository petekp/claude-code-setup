## Principle
Clearly communicate when content is AI-generated, what the AI's limitations are, and when users should apply their own judgment rather than trusting the output uncritically.

## Why It Matters
Users tend to trust computer-generated output as authoritative, especially when it is presented with confidence. AI systems, however, regularly produce plausible-sounding but incorrect, outdated, or biased outputs. Without transparency about these limitations, users make decisions based on flawed information, and the accountability gap between "the AI said so" and "a human decided" widens. Transparent AI design builds appropriate trust — users rely on AI where it is strong and apply critical judgment where it is weak.

## Application Guidelines
- Label AI-generated content clearly: "Generated by AI" or "AI-assisted" badges that persist with the content
- Include confidence indicators when the system can estimate them: "High confidence" vs. "This suggestion may need review"
- For factual claims, provide sources or indicate when sources are unavailable: "Based on data from Q3 2024" or "Unable to verify this claim"
- Communicate the AI's training cutoff or data freshness: "This recommendation is based on data through [date]"
- Provide an easy path to human review or override for any AI-generated decision: "Not right? Edit this" or "Flag for human review"
- When AI capabilities change or improve, communicate what has changed so users can update their calibration of when to trust the system

## Anti-Patterns
- Presenting AI-generated summaries, recommendations, or analysis with the same visual treatment as human-authored or verified content, with no indication that the information was machine-generated and may contain errors — implicitly claiming an accuracy level the system cannot guarantee
