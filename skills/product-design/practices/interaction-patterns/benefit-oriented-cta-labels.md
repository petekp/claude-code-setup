## Principle
Label call-to-action buttons with the benefit the user will receive or the task they will accomplish, not with generic verbs like "Submit," "OK," or "Continue."

## Why It Matters
Button labels are the final decision point before a user commits to an action. Generic labels ("Submit," "OK," "Next") tell users nothing about what will happen when they click. Benefit-oriented labels ("Create my account," "Send invitation," "Start free trial") confirm the user's intent, reduce anxiety about unknown consequences, and increase conversion rates. A/B testing consistently shows that specific, benefit-oriented labels outperform generic ones by meaningful margins, because they answer the user's implicit question: "What happens when I click this?"

## Application Guidelines
- Replace "Submit" with what actually happens: "Place order," "Send message," "Save changes," "Create project"
- Frame labels around the user's benefit when appropriate: "Start my free trial" rather than "Sign up," "Get my report" rather than "Download"
- Use first-person phrasing for commitment actions to increase ownership: "Create my account" rather than "Create account"
- For multi-step flows, make "Next" labels specific: "Continue to payment" or "Review your order" rather than just "Next"
- Keep labels concise (2-5 words) — they should be specific but not sentences
- Pair the primary CTA with a clear secondary action label: "Save and continue" / "Save as draft" rather than "OK" / "Cancel"

## Anti-Patterns
- Using "Submit" as the button label for every form in the application — whether it is creating an account, sending a message, filing a report, or placing an order — so users can never be sure what will happen when they click
