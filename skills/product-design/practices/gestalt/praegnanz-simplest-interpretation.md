## Principle
People perceive and interpret ambiguous or complex forms in the simplest way possible, so interfaces should present information in its most reduced, orderly arrangement.

## Why It Matters
The brain is wired to seek order and simplicity. When an interface presents unnecessarily complex layouts, overlapping elements, or visually cluttered arrangements, users expend extra effort parsing what they see. Designing toward Praegnanz means the first interpretation a user lands on is also the correct one, reducing errors and speeding comprehension.

## Application Guidelines
- Favor regular, symmetrical layouts over irregular compositions when both communicate the same information
- Reduce visual complexity by removing decorative elements that do not serve comprehension
- Use clear geometric primitives for icons and indicators rather than intricate or ambiguous illustrations
- When a layout could be interpreted multiple ways, add minimal structural cues (alignment, spacing) to collapse ambiguity into a single reading

## Anti-Patterns
- Overlapping cards or elements that create ambiguous depth relationships without clear z-order cues
- Using overly detailed or ornamental icons where a simple glyph would communicate the same meaning
- Designing asymmetrical dashboards where the eye cannot quickly find a stable, orderly structure
