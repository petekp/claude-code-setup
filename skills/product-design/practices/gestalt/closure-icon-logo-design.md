## Principle
Minimal icons and logos should leverage Closure — the brain's tendency to complete incomplete shapes — to communicate meaning with fewer visual elements, resulting in cleaner and more memorable marks.

## Why It Matters
Closure allows designers to remove portions of a shape while retaining recognizability, creating icons and logos that feel elegant rather than cluttered. Users actively participate in completing the form, which increases memorability and engagement. This principle is essential for designing marks that remain legible at very small sizes where every stroke counts.

## Application Guidelines
- Remove segments of a familiar shape strategically, ensuring enough of the contour remains for the brain to complete the rest — typically 60-70% of the outline is sufficient
- Use Closure to create meaningful negative space (e.g., the arrow in the FedEx logo) that adds a secondary layer of communication without additional visual weight
- Test Closure-based designs with users unfamiliar with the brand to verify the intended shape is perceived; do not assume your own pattern completion matches theirs
- Combine Closure with strong figure-ground relationships so the "missing" portions are clearly read as gaps in a form, not as additional shapes

## Anti-Patterns
- Removing so much of a shape that the intended form is no longer recoverable, resulting in an abstract mark that requires a legend to interpret
- Relying on Closure for critical UI icons (e.g., navigation, safety) where misinterpretation carries functional risk — these should be unambiguous
- Using inconsistent gap widths or positions across an icon set so the Closure cue feels accidental rather than intentional
