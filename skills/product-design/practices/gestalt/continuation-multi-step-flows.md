## Principle
Multi-step flows should use visual Continuation — a clear directional line or path — to convey sequence and progress, guiding the user's eye from completed steps through the current step toward remaining steps.

## Why It Matters
Users need to know where they are, where they have been, and where they are going. The Law of Continuation says the eye naturally follows a smooth path. A well-designed progress indicator exploits this to make the flow feel coherent and forward-moving, reducing anxiety about how much work remains and preventing users from abandoning mid-flow.

## Application Guidelines
- Connect step indicators with a continuous horizontal or vertical line so the eye follows the path from start to finish without interruption
- Differentiate completed, current, and upcoming segments of the line using color or opacity changes while keeping the line itself unbroken
- Place step labels consistently along the line (all above, or all below) to avoid breaking the Continuation path with misaligned text
- For long flows (more than five steps), consider grouping steps into phases with Continuation within each phase and clear phase boundaries between them

## Anti-Patterns
- Using disconnected step numbers or dots with no connecting line, which forces the user to mentally reconstruct the sequence
- Breaking the progress line with gaps, icons, or decorative elements that interrupt the eye's natural path
- Placing the progress indicator in inconsistent positions across steps (top of page on step one, sidebar on step two) so the Continuation cue is spatially broken
