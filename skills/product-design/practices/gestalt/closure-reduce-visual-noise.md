## Principle
The human visual system automatically completes incomplete shapes, so designers can remove redundant visual elements and let the brain fill in the gaps.

## Why It Matters
Every border, divider, and outline competes for attention. When you rely on closure, you strip away visual clutter without losing structural clarity. Users process the interface faster because their perception does the heavy lifting, reducing cognitive load and letting content breathe.

## Application Guidelines
- Remove full borders from cards and containers when alignment, whitespace, or partial edges already imply the boundary
- Use open-ended progress indicators or partial shapes to suggest continuation without drawing complete enclosures
- Replace solid divider lines between list items with consistent spacing alone, trusting the eye to perceive separation
- Test with users to confirm that implied boundaries are perceived correctly at all viewport sizes

## Anti-Patterns
- Adding full borders to every card, table cell, and section when spacing already communicates grouping
- Using heavy outlines on form fields that duplicate the closure already provided by background fills or underlines
- Removing so many visual cues that the implied shape becomes ambiguous and users cannot identify boundaries
