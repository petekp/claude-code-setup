## Principle
Evaluate interface designs against Jakob Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics as a structured, expert review method for identifying usability problems early and cheaply, before user testing.

## Why It Matters
Heuristic evaluation is one of the most cost-effective usability methods available. A single evaluator applying Nielsen's heuristics can identify 35% of usability issues; three to five evaluators can identify 75% or more. Unlike user testing, heuristic evaluation requires no participant recruitment, no test lab, and can be performed on prototypes, wireframes, or existing interfaces at any stage. It serves as a quality bar that every design should pass before reaching users.

## Application Guidelines
- **Visibility of system status:** The system should always keep users informed about what is going on through timely, appropriate feedback
- **Match between system and real world:** The system should speak the users' language and follow real-world conventions
- **User control and freedom:** Users need clearly marked "emergency exits" — undo, cancel, and back — to leave unwanted states
- **Consistency and standards:** Users should not have to wonder whether different words, situations, or actions mean the same thing
- **Error prevention:** Design to prevent errors from occurring in the first place, through constraints, defaults, and confirmations for critical actions
- **Recognition rather than recall:** Minimize memory load by making objects, actions, and options visible; instructions should be visible or easily retrievable
- **Flexibility and efficiency of use:** Accelerators for expert users that don't impede novice users
- **Aesthetic and minimalist design:** Interfaces should not contain irrelevant information; every extra unit of information competes with relevant information
- **Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors:** Error messages should be expressed in plain language, precisely indicate the problem, and constructively suggest a solution
- **Help and documentation:** Even though the system should be usable without documentation, it may be necessary to provide help that is easy to search, focused on the user's task, and concrete

## Anti-Patterns
- Treating heuristic evaluation as a replacement for user testing rather than a complement — it catches different types of issues
- Applying heuristics superficially (checking boxes) without deeply analyzing how each heuristic manifests in specific interface patterns
- Using heuristic evaluation only once at the end of development rather than iteratively throughout the design process
- Evaluating with a single reviewer, which catches significantly fewer issues than 3-5 independent evaluators comparing findings
