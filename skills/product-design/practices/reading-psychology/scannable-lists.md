## Principle
When content contains parallel items, sequential steps, or comparable options, present it as a formatted list rather than embedding it in paragraph prose.

## Why It Matters
Eye-tracking research shows that users scan rather than read on screens, processing as little as 20-28% of page text. Lists create visual entry points that let scanners extract key information without reading surrounding prose. Bulleted lists also leverage the isolation effect (Von Restorff) — items visually separated from running text are more memorable. When steps are numbered, users gain a mental model of progress and sequence that paragraph text cannot convey.

## Application Guidelines
- Use bullet lists for unordered sets of items where sequence doesn't matter (features, benefits, requirements)
- Use numbered lists when order matters: steps in a process, ranked priorities, or sequential instructions
- Keep list items grammatically parallel — if the first item starts with a verb, all items should start with a verb
- Limit lists to 5-9 items before grouping into sub-categories; beyond 9 items, scanning efficiency drops sharply
- Begin each list item with the most information-carrying word so users scanning the left edge absorb meaning quickly

## Anti-Patterns
- Burying three or more parallel items inside a comma-separated sentence within a paragraph, forcing users to re-read and mentally parse what a simple list would convey at a glance
