## Principle
Font selection is not merely aesthetic — typeface characteristics communicate personality, influence trust perception, and measurably affect reading speed and comprehension.

## Why It Matters
Research by Errol Morris in a New York Times experiment demonstrated that readers found identical statements more believable when set in Baskerville than in Comic Sans or Helvetica. Typography carries connotation: serif fonts signal tradition and authority, geometric sans-serifs signal modernity and efficiency, humanist sans-serifs signal approachability and warmth. Beyond perception, legibility varies dramatically between typefaces — fonts with larger x-heights, open counters, and distinct letterforms (especially l, I, 1, O, 0) reduce reading errors and increase speed.

## Application Guidelines
- Choose typefaces with large x-heights and open counters for body text — Inter, Source Sans, and Atkinson Hyperlegible are optimized for screen readability
- Match typeface personality to brand tone: a financial product benefits from serif authority (e.g., Georgia, Tiempos); a consumer app benefits from humanist warmth (e.g., Inter, Nunito)
- Limit the number of typefaces to 2 per product (one for headings, one for body) — more than 2 creates visual inconsistency
- Test typeface legibility at the actual sizes used in the product, especially for small text (captions, labels, footnotes) where legibility differences are most pronounced
- Avoid novelty or display typefaces for functional text; reserve them for marketing headers only

## Anti-Patterns
- Using a thin-weight fashion typeface (e.g., Didot Light at 13px) for body text in a data-heavy application — the hairline strokes and low x-height make extended reading exhausting and error-prone
