## Principle
Every link, button, and navigation label must provide enough semantic information for users to confidently predict what they will find after clicking.

## Why It Matters
Information foraging theory, developed by Peter Pirolli and Stuart Card at Xerox PARC, shows that users navigate interfaces the way animals forage for food — they follow "scent" cues that signal whether a path leads toward their goal. When scent is strong (a link clearly describes its destination), users navigate efficiently and confidently. When scent is weak or ambiguous ("Click here," "Learn more," "Resources"), users either hesitate, click speculatively and backtrack, or abandon the task. Poor information scent is the leading cause of navigation failure.

## Application Guidelines
- Write link text that describes the destination, not the action: "View pricing plans" not "Click here" — the link text should make sense out of context
- Use navigation labels that match the page titles they lead to; discrepancies between label and destination create distrust
- Include enough specificity in labels to differentiate between similar options: "Billing FAQ" and "Technical FAQ" not two links both labeled "FAQ"
- For CTAs, pair the verb with the object: "Download the Report" beats "Download" and "Start Free Trial" beats "Get Started"
- Test navigation labels by asking users to predict what they will find — if predictions are wrong, the scent is broken

## Anti-Patterns
- Using "Click here" or "Learn more" as link text throughout the interface, providing zero predictive value about the destination and forcing users to read surrounding context to determine what each link does
