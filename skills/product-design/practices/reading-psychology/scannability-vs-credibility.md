## Principle
Content must be scannable enough to respect users' time while providing enough depth to establish credibility and support informed decisions.

## Why It Matters
There is a real tension in content design: users want to scan quickly, but they also need enough information to feel confident in their decisions. Oversimplified content scans fast but feels thin and untrustworthy — "Is this all there is? What are they not telling me?" Overly detailed content feels credible but punishes scanners who abandon before reaching the key points. The solution is not choosing one extreme but designing content that works at multiple reading depths: scannable at the surface, substantive when explored.

## Application Guidelines
- Design content for two reading modes: a scanning layer (headings, bold phrases, bullet points) and a reading layer (full paragraphs with supporting evidence and nuance)
- Use expandable sections ("Read more," accordions) to offer depth without forcing it on scanners
- For high-stakes decisions (pricing, security, medical), provide both a summary and detailed supporting content — scanners get the gist, careful readers get the evidence
- Include specific numbers, data points, and concrete examples rather than vague claims — specificity builds credibility without requiring length
- Test with both scanning users and careful readers; optimize for neither at the expense of the other

## Anti-Patterns
- Stripping all content down to terse bullet points with no supporting context, creating a page that scans quickly but fails to answer the "why should I trust this?" question that drives conversion at decision points
