## Principle
The smallest text in an interface — button labels, tooltips, error messages, placeholder text — carries disproportionate influence over user behavior and emotional state.

## Why It Matters
Microcopy sits at decision points: the moment a user hesitates before clicking "Submit," reads an error message after a failed action, or glances at helper text before filling a field. Research consistently shows that changing a single word in a CTA can shift conversion rates by 10-30%. Beyond conversion, microcopy shapes emotional tone — a cold "Invalid input" feels punitive, while "That doesn't look like an email address — check for typos?" feels collaborative.

## Application Guidelines
- Write button labels that describe the outcome, not the action — "Get My Report" outperforms "Submit" because it frames the user's reward
- Front-load the most important word in every label; users scan the first 1-2 words and skip the rest
- Use error messages that name the problem, explain why it happened, and offer a fix: "Password must be 8+ characters. Add a number or symbol to strengthen it."
- Keep placeholder text as formatting hints, not labels — placeholders vanish on focus and cannot serve as persistent instructions
- Write confirmation dialogs that restate what will happen: "Delete 3 files permanently?" not "Are you sure?"

## Anti-Patterns
- Using generic, developer-facing labels like "Error 422" or "Null reference" in user-facing contexts — this forces users to interpret system internals and erodes trust
