## Principle
Present the most important information first — the conclusion, the answer, the key takeaway — then follow with supporting details in descending order of importance.

## Why It Matters
The inverted pyramid, borrowed from journalism, aligns perfectly with how users consume digital content. Studies consistently show that users read the first sentence or two of a block, then decide whether to continue. If the critical information is buried at the bottom of a paragraph, the majority of users will never reach it. Leading with the conclusion ensures that even users who read only the first line get the essential message. This pattern is especially critical for mobile, where screen real estate forces ruthless prioritization.

## Application Guidelines
- Start every paragraph, section, and page with the single most important piece of information a user needs from that content
- Structure help articles as: answer first, then context, then edge cases — not the reverse
- For notifications and alerts, put the action or status change in the first line, with details below
- In dashboards, surface the key metric or status at the top, with drill-down details available on demand
- Apply this to email and in-app messages too: the first sentence should carry the payload, not "We wanted to let you know that..."

## Anti-Patterns
- Writing long introductory paragraphs that provide background context before reaching the point — this buries the answer below the fold and forces users to wade through preamble to find what they need
