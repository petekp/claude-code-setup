## Principle
Structure pages so that a user scanning only the headings and subheadings can understand the full content architecture and navigate directly to the section they need.

## Why It Matters
Eye-tracking studies by the Nielsen Norman Group identified "layer-cake" scanning as a dominant pattern: users read headings, skip body text, read the next heading, skip more body text, and continue until they find the section relevant to their goal. If headings are vague ("Overview," "Details," "More Information"), the scanning pattern fails and users must read body text to orient — a far slower and more frustrating process. Well-crafted headings act as a table of contents embedded in the page itself.

## Application Guidelines
- Write headings that are specific and self-explanatory: "How to Reset Your Password" not "Getting Started" — a user should understand what is in the section without reading the body
- Use a consistent heading hierarchy (H1 > H2 > H3) with visual differentiation at each level; never skip levels
- Front-load keywords in headings — "Password Reset Instructions" scans faster than "Instructions for Resetting Your Password"
- Keep headings under 8 words where possible; concise headings scan faster and maintain visual rhythm
- Ensure sufficient vertical spacing above headings (more than below) so each heading visually "belongs to" its following content rather than floating between sections

## Anti-Patterns
- Using identical or near-identical headings across sections ("Information," "Details," "About") that force users to read body text to distinguish between them, defeating the entire scanning pattern
