## Principle
On text-heavy pages, users scan in an F-shaped pattern — reading the first line fully, scanning the second line partially, then scanning vertically down the left edge — so place the most important content along this path.

## Why It Matters
The F-pattern, documented extensively by the Nielsen Norman Group through eye-tracking studies, reveals that users do not read web pages uniformly. They invest the most reading effort in the top-left area and progressively less as they move down and to the right. Content placed in the lower-right of a text-heavy page receives the least attention. Understanding this pattern does not mean accepting it passively — good design works with the F-pattern where appropriate and breaks it intentionally (using visual hierarchy) when critical content would otherwise be missed.

## Application Guidelines
- Front-load the most important words at the beginning of headings, paragraphs, and list items — users scanning the left edge will catch them
- Place key information, CTAs, and navigation in the left two-thirds of text-heavy layouts
- Use the first two lines of any content block to deliver the most critical message, as these receive the most thorough reading
- Break the F-pattern intentionally with visual interrupts (images, callout boxes, contrasting sections) when important content must appear mid-page or on the right
- For data tables, put the most important column on the left; users scan the first column to find their row of interest

## Anti-Patterns
- Placing the primary call-to-action in the bottom-right corner of a text-heavy page without any visual emphasis, relying on users to read the entire page before finding the action — the F-pattern guarantees most users will never reach it
