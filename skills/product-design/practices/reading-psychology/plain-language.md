## Principle
Write interface text at the lowest complexity level that preserves accuracy, using common words and short sentences over technical jargon or formal language.

## Why It Matters
The average American adult reads at a 7th-8th grade level, and even highly educated users prefer plain language because it is faster to process. Cognitive fluency research shows that when text is easy to read, users perceive the product as more trustworthy, the task as easier, and the brand as more competent. Jargon does not signal expertise to users — it signals that the product was not designed for them. Every unfamiliar term creates a micro-interruption that breaks task flow.

## Application Guidelines
- Target a Flesch-Kincaid grade level of 6-8 for UI text; use the Hemingway Editor or similar readability tools to verify
- Replace Latinate words with Anglo-Saxon equivalents: "use" not "utilize," "start" not "initiate," "end" not "terminate"
- Define domain-specific terms inline on first use if they cannot be avoided, and provide a persistent glossary for complex products
- Write sentences under 20 words; if a sentence exceeds 25 words, split it
- Use active voice and second person ("You can export your data") rather than passive constructions ("Data can be exported by the user")

## Anti-Patterns
- Labeling a button "Authenticate Credentials" when "Log In" communicates the same action — using inflated language that adds syllables without adding meaning, creating unnecessary cognitive friction
