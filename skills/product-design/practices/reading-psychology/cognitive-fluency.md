## Principle
When information is easy to process — visually clean, linguistically simple, and well-structured — users perceive it as more truthful, more credible, and lower risk.

## Why It Matters
Cognitive fluency is a well-documented psychological bias: the subjective ease of processing information influences judgments about that information. Studies by Rolf Reber and Norbert Schwarz demonstrated that statements presented in high-contrast, legible fonts were rated as more truthful than identical statements in low-contrast or decorative fonts. In software interfaces, this means that readability directly affects trust. A product with clear typography, simple language, and logical layout is perceived as more reliable — not because of its features, but because the brain equates "easy to understand" with "safe and true." Conversely, interfaces that are hard to parse trigger skepticism and reduce engagement regardless of actual quality.

## Application Guidelines
- Optimize every readability dimension simultaneously: contrast, font size, line length, line height, plain language, and whitespace all contribute to fluency
- Use familiar visual patterns (standard form layouts, conventional navigation) — familiarity increases processing fluency
- Present pricing, permissions, and security information with maximum clarity; users are most skeptical at trust-sensitive decision points
- Avoid decorative fonts for functional text; serif or sans-serif body fonts with high x-heights process fastest
- Simplify sentence structure at critical decision points (checkout, account creation, permission grants) where trust is being evaluated
- Maintain consistent visual patterns so users can leverage learned expectations rather than reprocessing each screen

## Anti-Patterns
- Presenting terms of service or pricing details in tiny, low-contrast, dense text — this does not just reduce readability, it actively signals that the information is being hidden, triggering distrust
- Inconsistent button styles, link colors, or interaction patterns that force users to re-learn the interface on each screen
- Novel navigation patterns or unconventional layouts that sacrifice usability for visual novelty
- Dense walls of text in modals or tooltips that users must parse before taking action
