## Principle
On pages with minimal text and predominantly visual content (landing pages, hero sections, login screens), users scan in a Z-shaped pattern — design layouts to place key elements along this diagonal path.

## Why It Matters
The Z-pattern emerges when pages lack the dense text blocks that create F-pattern scanning. On visually-driven pages, the eye starts at the top-left (logo/brand), moves horizontally to the top-right (navigation/CTA), drops diagonally to the bottom-left (supporting content), and sweeps across to the bottom-right (primary action). This natural scanning path represents the user's attempt to quickly orient on a page and find the action point. Aligning your layout with this path means users find what they need faster, with less cognitive effort.

## Application Guidelines
- Place the brand/logo at the top-left (starting point) and primary navigation or secondary CTA at the top-right (first horizontal sweep)
- Position the headline or key value proposition along the diagonal or center of the page (the connecting sweep)
- Place the primary CTA at the bottom-right (terminal point) where the eye naturally comes to rest
- Use visual elements (images, icons, directional cues) along the diagonal to guide the eye from top-left toward bottom-right
- For pages with multiple Z-patterns stacked vertically (common in landing pages), each section should follow its own Z-rhythm

## Anti-Patterns
- Placing the primary CTA at the top-left of a hero section on a visually sparse page — users have not yet oriented themselves or read the value proposition, so the CTA appears before the motivation to click it has been established
