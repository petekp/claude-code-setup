## Principle
Place the most important decisions early in a workflow when users have the most cognitive energy, and minimize low-value decisions throughout.

## Why It Matters
Decision quality degrades over time as cognitive resources deplete — a phenomenon known as decision fatigue. Users who have already made dozens of minor choices (color preferences, notification settings, optional add-ons) have measurably less capacity for the critical decisions that follow (plan selection, payment commitment, access permissions). By front-loading high-stakes decisions and eliminating or automating trivial ones, you ensure users bring their best thinking to the choices that matter most.

## Application Guidelines
- Place the most consequential decision points (plan selection, core configuration) at the beginning of onboarding flows
- Use smart defaults to eliminate decisions that most users would make the same way
- Batch related minor decisions together rather than sprinkling them across multiple screens
- Offer "recommended" or "most popular" labels to reduce evaluation effort on non-critical choices
- Defer optional customization to after the core task is complete (e.g., "you can adjust these settings later")

## Anti-Patterns
- Forcing users through extensive preference configuration before they can use the product
- Placing the most important decision (e.g., pricing tier) at the end of a long onboarding flow
- Asking users to make decisions they don't yet have enough context to make well
- Presenting trivial and critical decisions with equal ceremony and visual weight
