## Principle
Design interfaces that consciously address the emotional dimension of user experience — building trust through transparency, creating safety through predictability and forgiveness, and generating appropriate moments of delight that reinforce positive engagement.

## Why It Matters
Don Norman's three levels of emotional design — visceral (immediate appearance), behavioral (pleasure of use), and reflective (self-image and meaning) — apply powerfully to enterprise software. Users who feel anxious about making mistakes work slower and avoid features. Users who don't trust the system create workarounds and shadow processes. Users who feel the tool reflects their professional competence engage more deeply. Emotional design isn't about making enterprise software "fun" — it's about removing fear, building confidence, and creating an environment where users feel safe to work efficiently.

## Application Guidelines
- Build trust through transparency: show what the system is doing, explain why decisions were made, and never hide errors or important information
- Create safety through forgiveness: undo, autosave, confirmation for irreversible actions, and clear recovery paths for mistakes
- Design predictable interactions: consistent behavior across the application so users feel in control and can anticipate outcomes
- Use micro-interactions purposefully: a subtle animation on successful save, a satisfying transition when completing a workflow, a celebratory moment when reaching a milestone
- Apply appropriate tone in system messages: empathetic error messages ("We couldn't process this — here's how to fix it"), encouraging progress messages ("Almost there — one more step")
- Respect the professional context: enterprise delight is subtle polish and efficiency, not confetti animations or casual language that undermines the seriousness of the work

## Anti-Patterns
- Enterprise software that feels hostile: unclear error messages, no forgiveness for mistakes, opaque system behavior that creates anxiety
- Over-designed delight that feels patronizing in a professional context: excessive animations, juvenile language, or gamification in serious workflows
- Interfaces that erode trust by hiding information, showing misleading status indicators, or silently failing
- Ignoring emotional design entirely, producing a technically functional but emotionally sterile experience that users tolerate but never enjoy
