## Principle
Give users genuine agency over their path through the product — meaningful choices about what to do next, how to configure their experience, and which goals to pursue create ownership and intrinsic motivation.

## Why It Matters
Self-determination theory identifies autonomy as a fundamental human need, and game design has proven that perceived choice — even between functionally similar options — dramatically increases engagement and satisfaction. When users feel they are choosing their path rather than being funneled through a predetermined corridor, they take more ownership of outcomes and invest more effort. The key word is "meaningful" — choices must have perceptible consequences to feel real. A choice between three functionally identical buttons is not autonomy; a choice between three genuinely different workflows is.

## Application Guidelines
- Offer multiple valid paths through onboarding based on user goals: "I want to manage projects," "I want to track time," "I want to collaborate" — each leading to a tailored first experience
- Let users choose the order of non-sequential tasks rather than enforcing a fixed sequence when order does not matter
- Provide customization options that have visible impact: choosing a theme, selecting a layout, picking a starting template
- When guiding users toward a recommended action, always present it as a suggestion alongside alternatives: "We recommend X, but you can also choose Y or Z"
- Design branching paths that converge on the same core competencies but let users arrive there through their preferred route

## Anti-Patterns
- Offering "choices" that are cosmetically different but functionally identical (three templates that look the same, multiple paths that all lead to the same mandatory step) — users quickly recognize fake autonomy and feel manipulated, producing the opposite of the intended engagement effect
