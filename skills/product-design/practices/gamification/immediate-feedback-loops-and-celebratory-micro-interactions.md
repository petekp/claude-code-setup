## Principle
Respond to every meaningful user action with immediate sensory feedback — visual, auditory, or haptic — and amplify milestone moments with celebratory micro-interactions that make achievement feel tangible.

## Why It Matters
Games are compelling because every input produces an immediate, satisfying output: a jump produces a sound and animation, a hit produces visual impact and screen shake, a level-up produces a cascade of effects. This tight feedback loop is what makes actions feel consequential. In product design, the same principle applies: a task marked complete should feel satisfying, a milestone reached should feel celebratory, and a successful submission should feel confirmed. Without feedback, users operate in a sensory vacuum where their actions feel weightless and unmemorable. The feedback does not need to be elaborate — it needs to be immediate, appropriate, and consistent.

## Application Guidelines
- Provide micro-animations for state changes: checkmarks that draw themselves, cards that slide into position, toggles that bounce slightly
- Celebrate milestone completions proportionally: first task complete gets a checkmark animation, first project complete gets confetti, first month active gets a personalized summary
- Use haptic feedback on mobile for confirmations and completions — even a subtle vibration reinforces that the action registered
- Keep celebratory moments brief (under 2 seconds) and non-blocking; the celebration should reward, not interrupt
- Scale feedback intensity with achievement significance: small actions get subtle responses, major milestones get unmistakable celebrations

## Anti-Patterns
- Triggering full-screen confetti animations for trivial actions like toggling a setting or clicking a checkbox — disproportionate celebration for minor actions feels patronizing and trains users to ignore feedback, which means they will also miss feedback for genuinely important events
