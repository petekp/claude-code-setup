## Principle
People disproportionately prefer guaranteed outcomes over probabilistic ones, even when the expected value of the uncertain option is higher — design for reliable small wins rather than unpredictable large rewards.

## Why It Matters
The certainty effect, a component of prospect theory, explains why people choose a guaranteed $50 over a 50% chance at $120 — even though the expected value of the gamble ($60) is higher. In product design, this means that guaranteed, predictable rewards (consistent daily value, reliable features, dependable performance) build stronger engagement than intermittent large rewards (occasional viral moments, sporadic big wins). Users who can count on a product to deliver value every session develop trust and habits; users who experience variable quality develop anxiety and eventually churn.

## Application Guidelines
- Ensure every session delivers a guaranteed minimum value — even a brief visit should produce something useful (a quick insight, a completed micro-task, a relevant notification)
- Make feature reliability the priority over feature novelty; a feature that works perfectly every time builds more trust than an impressive feature that works sometimes
- For reward systems, provide a guaranteed baseline reward with optional variable upside rather than purely variable rewards
- Show predictable time estimates: "This will take about 2 minutes" is preferred over uncertain waits, even if the uncertain wait averages shorter
- For SLAs and guarantees, emphasize the guaranteed minimum: "99.9% uptime guaranteed" is more compelling than "Average uptime of 99.99%"

## Anti-Patterns
- Designing engagement around variable reward schedules (random badge drops, unpredictable feature releases, inconsistent notification timing) without a reliable baseline of value delivery — this creates a slot-machine dynamic that attracts compulsive usage but not genuine satisfaction
