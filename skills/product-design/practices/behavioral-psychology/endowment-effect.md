## Principle
People value things more once they feel ownership over them — let users create, customize, and personalize within the product early so they develop psychological ownership before any purchase decision.

## Why It Matters
The endowment effect, demonstrated by Kahneman, Knetsch, and Thaler, shows that people assign higher value to things they own than to identical things they do not own. In software, ownership is psychological, not legal — a user who has customized a dashboard, created content, or configured a workspace feels that it is "theirs" even during a free trial. This sense of ownership makes the prospect of losing it (by not upgrading, by canceling) feel like a genuine loss. Products that enable creation and customization during onboarding convert free users to paid at significantly higher rates. However, this also creates strong resistance to migrations, redesigns, and even upgrades that would improve their experience — transitions must be handled with extreme care.

## Application Guidelines
- Let users personalize their experience early: choose a theme, set a name, upload a logo, configure a workspace — these acts of creation build ownership
- Enable content creation during free trials rather than limiting to read-only access; users who create content have something to lose
- Use possessive language: "Your dashboard," "Your workspace," "Your team" — linguistic ownership reinforces psychological ownership
- Show users their accumulated value: "You've created 12 documents this week" reminds them of what they have built
- During upgrade prompts, reference their specific customizations and content rather than generic feature lists
- When migrating users to new systems, carry over their customizations visibly — show them their data survived the transition
- When sunsetting features, give users export tools and ample transition time rather than abrupt removal

## Anti-Patterns
- Running a free trial that disables customization and creation, forcing users to experience the product as passive observers — this prevents the endowment effect from forming and makes the trial feel like a demo rather than a product the user owns
- Resetting user customizations during updates without warning or migration
- Forcing users onto a "better" new version that discards their accumulated configurations
- Removing features users have built workflows around without providing equivalent alternatives
- Treating user-generated content as disposable during platform changes
