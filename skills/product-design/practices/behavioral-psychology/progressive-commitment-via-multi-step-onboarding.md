## Principle
Structure onboarding as a series of small, easy commitments that gradually increase in effort — users who complete small initial steps are psychologically invested and more likely to complete larger subsequent steps.

## Why It Matters
The foot-in-the-door technique, validated across decades of social psychology research, shows that people who agree to a small request are significantly more likely to agree to a larger one. In product onboarding, this means that asking for a name and email first (low effort), then workspace configuration (medium effort), then team invitations (higher effort) produces better completion rates than presenting all steps at once. Each completed micro-commitment creates cognitive consistency pressure: the user thinks "I have already invested in this, so I should continue."

## Application Guidelines
- Start with the lowest-effort, highest-reward step — let users experience value before asking for investment (name and email before billing, content creation before team setup)
- Break complex setup into 3-5 discrete steps with visible progress; never present a single screen with 15 fields
- Defer optional steps: collect only what is essential now and offer "complete your profile" prompts later
- Celebrate each step completion with micro-feedback (checkmarks, progress advancement, encouraging copy) to reinforce the investment
- Allow skipping non-essential steps — forced completion of irrelevant steps creates resentment, not commitment

## Anti-Patterns
- Requiring credit card information as the first step of a free trial onboarding flow — this is the highest-friction ask placed at the lowest-trust moment, inverting the progressive commitment principle and maximizing abandonment
