## Principle
The way choices are presented — their order, framing, defaults, and context — predictably influences which option users select; design choice architecture to guide users toward outcomes that benefit them.

## Why It Matters
Thaler and Sunstein's nudge theory demonstrates that there is no such thing as neutral choice presentation. Every design decision — which option is first, which is pre-selected, which is visually emphasized — is an act of choice architecture that steers behavior. A "recommended" badge, a visual highlight on one pricing tier, or the order of options in a dropdown all function as nudges. Since the architecture will influence behavior regardless, the ethical imperative is to design it deliberately so the nudge direction aligns with the user's best interest, not merely the business's revenue goals.

## Application Guidelines
- Highlight the recommended option with visual emphasis (badge, color, size) when one option genuinely serves most users best
- Order choices from simplest/most common to most complex/specialized, so users encounter the most likely answer first
- Use smart defaults based on user context (location, device, role) rather than one-size-fits-all selections
- Remove or de-emphasize options that are rarely appropriate — showing them at equal weight with common options introduces unnecessary decision cost
- Make the nudge transparent: label recommended options with the reason ("Most popular," "Best for teams under 10") so users understand the basis for the suggestion

## Anti-Patterns
- Visually emphasizing the most expensive pricing tier by making it larger and more colorful while making the free tier text small and gray — this uses choice architecture to steer users toward a purchase that may not serve them, not toward their best outcome
