## Principle
People judge an experience primarily by its most intense moment (the peak) and its final moment (the end) — deliberately design positive peaks and satisfying endings into every user session.

## Why It Matters
Kahneman's peak-end rule demonstrates that the overall duration of an experience matters surprisingly little to how it is remembered — what matters is the emotional highpoint and the conclusion. A 45-minute session with a frustrating middle but a delightful ending is remembered more favorably than a smooth 30-minute session with a flat conclusion. In product design, this means that engineering one moment of delight and ensuring the session ends on a positive note can transform how users feel about the entire experience. This directly affects retention, NPS scores, and word-of-mouth. Duration neglect means that uniformly improving every step by a small amount is less effective than investing disproportionately in peaks and endings.

## Application Guidelines
- Identify and engineer at least one "peak moment" in key workflows — a satisfying animation when a task completes, a personalized insight after data entry, or a milestone acknowledgment
- End sessions positively: show a summary of accomplishments, a motivating statistic, or a gentle "Welcome back" on return
- Front-load unpleasant tasks (form filling, configuration) and end with rewarding ones (seeing results, reviewing achievements)
- Design error recovery to end on resolution: after fixing an issue, show a clear success state rather than just removing the error
- For long workflows (onboarding, checkout), place the most rewarding step last — the completion celebration should be genuinely satisfying
- Identify the "peak" moments in your key flows (the moment of commitment, the moment of first value, the moment of completion) and polish them disproportionately
- Invest heavily in the final step of any flow: the confirmation screen, the success state, the post-purchase experience
- Avoid ending any flow on an error or ambiguous state — if the last thing a user sees is confusing, that becomes their lasting impression
- After support interactions, follow up with a warm closing message rather than ending on the resolution of the complaint

## Anti-Patterns
- Ending a user's session with a confusing error, an upsell popup, or an abrupt timeout — the last impression disproportionately colors the memory of the entire experience
- Redirecting users to a blank dashboard after completing a 15-minute onboarding flow with no confirmation, no summary, and no guidance on what to do next
- Checkout flows that end with a bland confirmation page after an otherwise well-designed shopping experience
- Support interactions that resolve the issue but end with a cold survey request
- Onboarding sequences where the final step is the most complex or frustrating
- Products that deliver a great first-run experience but have no investment in the ongoing or exit experience
