## Principle
A user's emotional state at the moment of decision-making significantly affects the quality and type of decisions they make — design the experience leading to critical decisions to promote a calm, confident, informed emotional state.

## Why It Matters
Affective forecasting research shows that mood systematically biases decision-making. Anxious users make risk-averse, conservative choices (and often avoid deciding altogether). Frustrated users make impulsive choices to escape the situation. Confident, calm users make more deliberate, higher-quality decisions. In product design, this means that the experience immediately preceding a decision point — an upgrade prompt, a configuration choice, a data deletion — directly influences the decision quality. If the path to the decision was frustrating, confusing, or anxiety-inducing, the decision itself will reflect that emotional state.

## Application Guidelines
- Ensure the user experience leading to upgrade/purchase decisions is smooth and positive — a user who just had a delightful product experience is in the optimal emotional state to evaluate an upgrade
- Never place critical decisions immediately after error states or frustrating experiences; allow emotional recovery first
- Reduce anxiety at high-stakes decision points (checkout, data migration, account changes) with clear information, reversibility assurances, and calm visual design
- Use warm, supportive copy at decision points: "You can change this anytime" reduces the anxiety that leads to decision avoidance
- Avoid time pressure at complex decisions — countdown timers and "limited time" urgency push users toward impulsive choices they may regret

## Anti-Patterns
- Presenting an upgrade upsell immediately after a user encounters a frustrating limitation or error ("Hit your storage limit? Upgrade now!") — the user's emotional state is frustration, which produces resentful compliance rather than genuine purchase satisfaction, increasing refund rates and churn
