## Principle
People are more engaged, more satisfied, and more committed when they feel in control of their experience — give users meaningful choices and respect their agency at every interaction.

## Why It Matters
Self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan) identifies autonomy as one of three fundamental psychological needs. When users feel that a product is doing things to them rather than for them, engagement drops and resistance rises. This manifests as hostility toward forced updates, resentment of removed features, and rage-clicks on unskippable modals. Conversely, users who feel in control — who can customize their experience, choose their workflow, and override defaults — report higher satisfaction and lower churn, even when the product is objectively less efficient than a more prescriptive alternative.

## Application Guidelines
- Make all non-critical interruptions dismissible: modals, tooltips, banners, and onboarding steps should always have a clear close or skip option
- Provide "Undo" instead of "Are you sure?" — undo preserves flow while respecting the user's right to reverse decisions
- Let users customize their workspace: reorder navigation, choose display density, set notification preferences, configure keyboard shortcuts
- When automating actions (auto-categorization, smart suggestions), show the automation's work and let users override it
- Avoid dark patterns that remove control: hidden unsubscribe links, pre-checked consent boxes, and forced phone number collection all violate autonomy

## Anti-Patterns
- Forcing users through a multi-step product tour with no skip option, trapping them in an experience they did not choose and cannot exit — this violates autonomy and creates negative associations with the product before the user has done anything voluntarily
