## Principle
People can process and compare information more effectively when it is organized into meaningful chunks of roughly five to seven items.

## Why It Matters
George Miller's research established that organizing information into meaningful groups dramatically improves comprehension and retention. In interfaces, unchunked information — a wall of form fields, an undifferentiated list of settings, a paragraph-dense help page — overwhelms users and causes them to miss critical items. Proper chunking transforms cognitive overload into manageable scanning, enabling users to find what they need quickly and understand the relationships between items.

## Application Guidelines
- Group related form fields into labeled sections of 5-7 fields each, separated by clear visual dividers or whitespace
- Organize navigation menus into categorical groups, with each group containing no more than 7 items
- When presenting feature lists, pricing tiers, or comparison tables, chunk attributes into thematic categories
- Break long-form content into sections with descriptive headings, each covering a single cohesive topic
- Format phone numbers, account numbers, and other long strings into hyphenated or spaced groups (e.g., 555-867-5309, not 5558675309)
- In settings pages, group related toggles and options under clear subheadings rather than presenting a single flat list

## Anti-Patterns
- A settings page with 30 toggles in a single undivided list, requiring users to scan every item linearly
- A sidebar navigation with 15 top-level items and no grouping or visual hierarchy
- Displaying a 16-digit number as a single unbroken string
- A form with 20 fields in a single column with no section breaks, headings, or logical grouping
