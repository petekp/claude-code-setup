## Principle
When presented with too many options simultaneously, people experience anxiety, decision fatigue, and reduced satisfaction with their eventual choice.

## Why It Matters
The paradox of choice is well-documented: more options feel like more freedom but actually produce worse outcomes. The famous jam study (Iyengar and Lepper) showed that 24 varieties attracted more browsers but 6 varieties drove ten times more purchases. Users faced with overwhelming choices often defer the decision entirely (abandoning the task), make a suboptimal snap choice to escape the discomfort, or choose and then regret. In product design, this manifests as lower conversion rates, higher abandonment, and increased support requests from users who are unsure they chose correctly. Curation is a design service: it communicates "We have evaluated these options so you don't have to."

## Application Guidelines
- For plan/pricing selection, offer three tiers with a clearly recommended default — avoid presenting more than four; research consistently shows that 3 is optimal for comparison without overwhelm
- In template or theme selectors, show a curated set of 6-8 popular options with a "Browse all" escape hatch
- Use progressive filtering (category, then subcategory, then items) to narrow large catalogs rather than displaying everything at once
- When users must select from a long list, provide a recommended or "most popular" subset prominently, with search for the rest
- Implement smart defaults that eliminate the choice entirely for users who do not need customization
- For permission/role assignment, offer preset bundles (Admin, Editor, Viewer) rather than exposing every individual permission
- Replace open-ended choices with constrained choices where possible: "Choose from these 5 colors" is easier than "Enter any hex code"
- In settings, separate "common" from "advanced" settings rather than presenting everything in one flat list

## Anti-Patterns
- A template gallery that dumps 200 templates in a grid with no categorization, filtering, or recommendations
- A settings page that surfaces every possible configuration option without distinguishing common from advanced
- An onboarding flow that asks users to choose from 15 product use-cases before they have had a chance to explore the product
- Presenting all available integrations in an alphabetical flat list rather than categorized by function or popularity
- Pricing pages with more than 3-4 tiers, each with subtle differences that require careful comparison
