## Principle
Every field in a form must earn its place by directly serving the user's goal or a critical business requirement — all others should be removed.

## Why It Matters
Each additional form field increases abandonment probability. Research by Baymard Institute shows that checkout abandonment increases measurably with every unnecessary field. Beyond completion rates, unnecessary fields signal to users that the product values data collection over their time. Shorter forms also reduce error rates, support time, and data storage costs. The question for every field should be: "What happens if we don't collect this?"

## Application Guidelines
- Audit every field by asking: Is this required to complete the user's immediate task? Can it be derived, inferred, or collected later?
- Combine fields where possible: use a single "Full Name" field instead of separate first/middle/last fields unless legal or system requirements demand separation
- Defer non-essential fields to post-completion flows (e.g., collect profile photo and bio after account creation, not during signup)
- Use progressive profiling — collect additional data over time across multiple interactions rather than front-loading everything into one form
- Mark truly optional fields clearly (with "(optional)" text, not asterisks on required fields) so users know they can skip them
- Eliminate fields that collect data the system already has (e.g., don't ask for email if the user is logged in)

## Anti-Patterns
- Including fields like "How did you hear about us?" or "Company size" in a signup form where the user's only goal is to create an account and start using the product, adding friction that benefits internal analytics at the cost of conversions
