## Principle
The first piece of information users encounter disproportionately influences all subsequent judgments — making the initial number, price, or reference point a powerful framing tool.

## Why It Matters
When users see a "was $199, now $99" price, the $199 anchors their perception of value regardless of whether the product was ever actually worth $199. When a slider defaults to a certain value, users adjust from that anchor rather than evaluating independently. When a dashboard opens showing this month's metrics, those numbers anchor the user's assessment of trends. Anchoring is among the most robust cognitive biases — it works even when people know about it, even when the anchor is arbitrary, and even when they are told to ignore it.

## Application Guidelines
- In pricing interfaces, show the original or reference price alongside discounted prices to anchor value perception
- Set form field defaults and slider positions thoughtfully — they serve as anchors that most users will adjust from rather than replace
- When presenting ranges (estimates, timelines, budgets), lead with the number you want to anchor: "typically 3-7 days" anchors differently than "up to 7 days"
- In negotiation or bidding interfaces, the first visible number sets the range for all subsequent numbers
- Use anchoring ethically in donation interfaces: suggesting specific amounts ("$25, $50, $100") anchors giving levels higher than an open text field

## Anti-Patterns
- Displaying artificially inflated "original prices" that create false value perceptions
- Setting slider defaults at maximum values to anchor users toward higher quantities or prices
- Showing competitors' high prices as anchors to make your own overpriced product seem reasonable by comparison
- Using extreme initial estimates to make actual (still unreasonable) numbers seem acceptable ("We thought it would take 6 months — great news, only 4!")
