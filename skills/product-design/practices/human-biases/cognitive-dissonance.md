## Principle
When users encounter information or experiences that conflict with their beliefs, expectations, or past actions, they experience psychological discomfort that they will work to reduce — often by rejecting the new information rather than changing their behavior.

## Why It Matters
Users who just purchased a product will dismiss negative reviews to justify their decision. Users who invested time learning a complex workflow will resist simpler alternatives because adopting them would mean admitting the earlier effort was wasted. When interfaces create dissonance — by contradicting expectations, breaking established patterns, or questioning past choices — users respond with rationalization, avoidance, or hostility rather than reasoned adaptation. Understanding this allows designers to introduce change in ways that minimize dissonance rather than triggering defensive reactions.

## Application Guidelines
- After purchase or signup, reinforce the user's decision with confirmation screens, success messaging, and social proof ("Join 50,000 others who chose this plan")
- When introducing changes to established workflows, frame them as enhancements to what users already do rather than replacements that invalidate their current knowledge
- Provide gradual transitions rather than abrupt changes so users can integrate new patterns without feeling their expertise has been made obsolete
- When delivering unwelcome information (usage limits, declining performance), pair it with actionable next steps that give users agency
- Align marketing promises with actual product experience to prevent post-purchase dissonance

## Anti-Patterns
- Radically redesigning interfaces without migration paths, forcing users to confront that their learned expertise is now worthless
- Displaying contradictory information in different parts of the product (one screen says "free" while another shows charges)
- Shaming users for past behavior while asking them to change (e.g., "You have been wasting 3 hours a week — try our tool")
- Post-purchase interfaces that surface buyer's remorse triggers like cheaper alternatives or negative reviews
