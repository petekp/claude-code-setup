## Principle
A positive impression in one area of an interface causes users to assume positive qualities in unrelated areas — a beautiful design is assumed to be functional, a fast feature is assumed to be reliable.

## Why It Matters
The halo effect means that investments in one highly visible quality (visual design, speed, brand reputation) create a glow that extends to qualities users have not actually evaluated (security, data accuracy, support quality). A polished landing page makes users assume the product behind it is equally polished. A fast initial load time makes users assume all subsequent operations will be fast. This works in reverse too: one bad experience creates a negative halo that taints everything else. The implication is that first impressions and most-visible qualities disproportionately determine overall product perception.

## Application Guidelines
- Invest disproportionately in the quality of first-touch surfaces (landing page, onboarding, first action) because they create halos that color everything after
- Ensure that your most visible feature is also your most polished — the halo radiates from what users see most
- When one area of the product has quality issues, prevent negative halo by isolating the experience ("We are improving this section" messaging)
- Use brand consistency to extend positive associations from strong brand elements to weaker product areas
- In product launches, lead with your strongest feature to create a positive halo that carries users through less developed areas

## Anti-Patterns
- Investing heavily in marketing and landing pages while neglecting in-product experience, creating a trust-destroying gap when the halo breaks
- Allowing one buggy feature to exist alongside polished ones without acknowledgment, letting the negative halo spread
- Relying on brand reputation to excuse poor product quality, assuming the halo will hold indefinitely
- Designing a stunning visual interface that is functionally broken, exploiting the halo to delay users' discovery of quality problems
