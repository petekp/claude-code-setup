## Principle
When users feel their freedom of choice is being restricted or manipulated, they react by doing the opposite of what is being pushed — making aggressive persuasion tactics counterproductive.

## Why It Matters
Mandatory modals, forced tutorials, manipulative copy ("Are you sure? Only losers skip this offer"), and interfaces that remove options all trigger reactance. Users do not just ignore the push — they actively resist it. A forced pop-up to download the app makes users less likely to download it. A guilt-tripping unsubscribe flow makes users angrier at the brand. A mandatory onboarding tour makes users click through without reading. The harder an interface pushes, the harder users push back, often permanently damaging the relationship.

## Application Guidelines
- Always provide a clear, easy way to dismiss, skip, or close any interruptive element
- Frame suggestions as options, not mandates: "You might find this useful" instead of "You must complete this step"
- Avoid manipulative opt-out language ("No thanks, I do not want to save money") that shames users for exercising choice
- Let users control the pace and sequence of onboarding rather than forcing a prescribed path
- When requiring an action for legitimate reasons, explain why it is necessary rather than simply blocking progress

## Anti-Patterns
- Full-screen modals with no visible close button or dismiss option
- "Confirmshaming" — opt-out buttons with guilt-laden text designed to manipulate the user's choice
- Forced app download prompts that block access to web content
- Mandatory multi-step tutorials that cannot be skipped, teaching users to resent the onboarding process
- Pop-ups that reappear immediately after being dismissed
