## Principle
Users value things they helped create far more than equivalent pre-made alternatives — even minimal personal effort invested in building or customizing something significantly increases attachment and perceived value.

## Why It Matters
When users assemble their own dashboard, configure their own workflow, or build their own report, they value the result more than an identical pre-built version. This is distinct from the endowment effect — it is specifically about labor investment creating love. The effort must be meaningful but not excessive: too little effort and the effect does not activate; too much effort and users give up before completing. The sweet spot is guided creation where users feel they built something but were supported through the process.

## Application Guidelines
- Design onboarding flows where users make meaningful choices that visibly shape the product to their needs (choosing data sources, naming workspaces, selecting themes)
- Let users customize and assemble their own views, reports, and workflows rather than only offering pre-built options
- When providing templates, require users to make a few personalization choices rather than delivering a completely finished product
- Ensure the creation process is completable — the IKEA effect requires successful completion, so provide enough guidance that users finish
- Show users the tangible output of their effort throughout the creation process to maintain motivation

## Anti-Patterns
- Offering only pre-built, non-customizable experiences that give users no opportunity to invest effort
- Making customization so difficult that users give up before completing, triggering frustration rather than pride
- Auto-configuring everything without user input, sacrificing attachment for efficiency
- Allowing extensive customization but then resetting or discarding it during updates, compounding the loss with betrayal of invested effort
