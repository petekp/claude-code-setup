## Principle
Introducing a strategically inferior third option can shift user preference between two existing options — an asymmetrically dominated "decoy" makes the target option look decisively better by comparison.

## Why It Matters
When users choose between two options that are different along multiple dimensions (price vs. features, speed vs. thoroughness), the choice is difficult because neither clearly dominates. Introducing a third option that is clearly worse than one of the originals but comparable to the other breaks the deadlock — it makes the dominating option look superior not just to the decoy but to the other real option as well. This is why pricing pages often include a tier that seems oddly positioned: it exists to make the target tier look like the obvious choice.

## Application Guidelines
- In pricing page design, include a tier that is slightly worse than the target tier on all dimensions to make the target the obvious "best value"
- When presenting plan comparisons, ensure the decoy is close enough to the target to create relevant comparison but clearly inferior
- Use the decoy effect to help users make decisions they would be satisfied with — the decoy should steer toward genuinely good options, not exploitative ones
- In feature comparison matrices, the positioning of options relative to each other matters as much as the absolute features of each option
- Test decoy placement and pricing carefully — the effect is sensitive to the specific relationships between options

## Anti-Patterns
- Creating decoy tiers that are so obviously bad they insult the user's intelligence and signal manipulation
- Using the decoy effect to steer users toward options that genuinely do not serve their needs
- Adding confusing filler plans that create choice overload rather than clarifying the decision
- Making the decoy so similar to the target that users cannot tell them apart, creating confusion instead of clarity
