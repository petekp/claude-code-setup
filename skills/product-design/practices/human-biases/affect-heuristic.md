## Principle
Users make judgments and decisions based on their current emotional state rather than objective analysis — if an interface feels good, users judge it as good, safe, and trustworthy.

## Why It Matters
Emotional responses to an interface precede and dominate rational evaluation. A beautifully designed product is perceived as more functional, more reliable, and more secure — even before the user tests any of those qualities. Conversely, an interface that triggers negative emotions (frustration, confusion, anxiety) causes users to judge the underlying product as lower quality regardless of its actual capabilities. This means aesthetic quality, microcopy tone, and interaction polish are not superficial concerns — they directly influence perceived functionality, trustworthiness, and willingness to pay.

## Application Guidelines
- Invest in visual polish and interaction quality for first-impression screens — they set the emotional baseline for the entire experience
- Use warm, encouraging microcopy in error states and empty states to maintain positive affect when things go wrong
- Design loading states and transitions that feel smooth and intentional rather than janky or uncertain
- Celebrate user accomplishments with satisfying feedback (animations, success messages) to associate positive emotions with the product
- Test emotional responses alongside functional usability — ask users how the interface makes them feel, not just whether they can use it

## Anti-Patterns
- Neglecting visual design quality because "users care about functionality" — they care about both, and affect influences perceived functionality
- Using cold, technical error messages that create negative emotional associations with the product
- Jarring transitions, layout shifts, and visual inconsistencies that trigger subconscious discomfort
- Onboarding flows that front-load frustrating steps (verification, permissions) before the user has any positive emotional connection to the product
