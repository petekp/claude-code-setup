## Principle
Users assign higher value to things that are scarce, limited, or time-constrained — and the fear of missing out on something available can be more motivating than the desire for something guaranteed.

## Why It Matters
Limited-time offers, low-stock warnings, exclusive access, and expiring invitations all leverage the same cognitive mechanism: scarcity makes things feel more valuable and creates urgency to act before the opportunity disappears. In digital products, scarcity manifests as limited beta slots, time-limited discounts, exclusive features for early adopters, and countdown timers. When scarcity is real, communicating it is informative and helpful. When scarcity is fabricated, it is manipulative and eventually destroys trust when users realize the "limited offer" is permanent.

## Application Guidelines
- Communicate genuine scarcity honestly: actual remaining inventory, real deadlines, true capacity limits
- Use urgency cues for time-sensitive actions where delay has real consequences (expiring discounts, limited event seats)
- For exclusive features or early access, clearly communicate what makes them exclusive and when general availability will follow
- Design FOMO-aware social features that show what others are doing or achieving to motivate participation without creating anxiety
- Use scarcity ethically as a decision-making aid ("This price ends Friday" for a real promotion) rather than as a pressure tactic

## Anti-Patterns
- Perpetual "limited time offers" that never actually expire, training users to ignore urgency cues
- Fake low-stock warnings ("Only 2 left!") for digital products with unlimited inventory
- Countdown timers that reset when they expire, revealing the urgency was manufactured
- Creating artificial exclusivity that serves no purpose other than making users feel pressured to act immediately
