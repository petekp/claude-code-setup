## Principle
Users evaluate options not by their absolute qualities but by comparison with adjacent alternatives — meaning the context in which you present a choice shapes perception as much as the choice itself.

## Why It Matters
A pricing tier looks expensive in isolation but reasonable next to a premium option. A loading time feels fast after a slow competitor but sluggish after an instant one. Users do not evaluate interfaces in a vacuum; they compare against what they just saw, what sits beside it, and what they expected. This makes the sequencing and juxtaposition of elements a powerful design lever — and a dangerous one if misused to manipulate rather than clarify.

## Application Guidelines
- In pricing pages, include a higher-tier option that makes the target tier feel like good value by comparison
- When showing before/after states (e.g., optimization results), present them side by side to amplify perceived improvement
- Sequence onboarding steps from harder to easier so progress feels increasingly effortless
- Use contrast intentionally in feature comparison tables — highlight differences, not just features
- When redesigning interfaces, show the old and new side by side during transition to help users appreciate improvements

## Anti-Patterns
- Placing a mediocre option next to a terrible one to make it look good, rather than genuinely improving the offering
- Using dark patterns where a "free" tier is deliberately crippled to make paid tiers look necessary by contrast
- Inconsistent visual contrast that makes important elements blend with their surroundings
- Showing users their data usage next to an unrealistically high limit to create false comfort
