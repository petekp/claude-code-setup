## Principle
Users give disproportionate weight to information and recommendations from perceived authorities — including brands, certifications, expert endorsements, and the product interface itself.

## Why It Matters
When a trusted brand recommends a setting, users accept it without evaluation. When an interface presents a suggestion as an "expert recommendation" or "best practice," users follow it uncritically. Authority signals — professional design, recognized logos, certifications, expert quotes — short-circuit deliberative evaluation. This makes authority cues powerful tools for building trust and guiding behavior, but also means interfaces have an outsized responsibility not to abuse that trust. Users treat the interface as an authority, which means design choices function as implicit recommendations.

## Application Guidelines
- Display relevant certifications, security badges, and compliance indicators where they reduce legitimate user concerns (checkout, data entry)
- Use expert endorsements and professional testimonials for complex products where users need external validation
- When the product makes recommendations ("Recommended for you," "Best value"), ensure they are genuinely in the user's interest
- Maintain visual professionalism — polished design signals competence and trustworthiness through authority association
- Cite sources and provide transparency when making claims, so authority is earned rather than merely asserted

## Anti-Patterns
- Displaying fake or expired security badges to manufacture trust
- Using "recommended" labels on options that benefit the business rather than the user
- Leveraging professional visual design to mask predatory terms or misleading pricing
- Name-dropping partnerships or certifications that are irrelevant to the user's concern
