## Principle
Users look to the behavior and choices of others — especially peers and perceived authorities — as evidence of what is correct, safe, or desirable, particularly in situations of uncertainty.

## Why It Matters
When users are unsure whether to trust a product, complete a purchase, or adopt a feature, they look for signals that others have done so successfully. Review counts, user testimonials, "most popular" badges, real-time activity indicators, and community size metrics all reduce perceived risk and increase conversion. This is not herd mentality — it is a rational heuristic for navigating uncertainty. However, fabricated or misleading social proof destroys trust catastrophically when discovered.

## Application Guidelines
- Display genuine usage metrics where they reduce uncertainty: user counts, review scores, download numbers
- Show real-time activity indicators ("12 people viewing this right now") to create a sense of community and urgency
- Use testimonials from users who match your target audience demographically and situationally
- Highlight "most popular" or "most chosen" options on pricing and plan selection pages
- In collaborative products, show team adoption metrics ("8 of 10 team members have activated") to encourage remaining holdouts
- Show real-time activity feeds ("Sarah from Denver just signed up") to create a sense of momentum and community
- Use peer-specific proof — segment testimonials by industry, role, or company size so users see validation from people like them
- Display aggregate ratings with review counts: "4.7 stars from 2,341 reviews" is more persuasive than "4.7 stars" alone because volume signals reliability
- Prioritize authentic, specific testimonials over polished generic ones — "Reduced our deployment time from 4 hours to 20 minutes" beats "Great product!"

## Anti-Patterns
- Fabricating testimonials, inflating user counts, or using stock photos for "real user" reviews
- Showing social proof that inadvertently highlights low adoption ("Join our 12 users!")
- Using social proof from irrelevant demographics (Fortune 500 logos for a product targeting freelancers)
- Displaying real-time activity counts that reveal embarrassingly low engagement during off-peak hours
